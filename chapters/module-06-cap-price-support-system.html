Putting It All Together: The CAP Price Support System

A full market analysis of price supports, government purchases, fiscal cost, and welfare consequences in the EU sugar market.

Learning Objectives
  • Use demand, supply, and equilibrium tools to analyze a price support policy
  • Explain how a government purchase commitment acts as a policy-driven demand shifter
  • Calculate the surplus created by a supported price above equilibrium
  • Separate producer gains, private consumer losses, fiscal cost, and efficiency implications
  • Apply welfare analysis to evaluate the CAP price support system

Introduction

Modules 1-5 built the tools one layer at a time. You learned how to construct demand and supply curves, locate equilibrium, analyze shifters, and measure consumer surplus, producer surplus, social surplus, and deadweight loss. This module puts those tools together in one policy application: the CAP price support system.

The question is no longer only whether the policy raises the price farmers receive. A full market analysis asks who gains, who loses, what the government must pay for, and what efficiency costs may result when policy moves the market away from the competitive equilibrium.

Policy Connection: CAP as a Market Shifter

The EU's Common Agricultural Policy illustrates demand and supply analysis in action. Supporting high prices through government purchases created large agricultural surpluses and changed who gained and who lost in the EU sugar market. In this module, we examine the effect of CAP on the EU market as a demand shifter and then connect that shift to welfare analysis.

Price Support: A Demand Shifter in the EU Market

CAP's price support mechanism guaranteed farmers a minimum price for their output. Unlike the simple price floor we explored in Module 3, price support involves governments purchasing any unsold surplus at the supported price if market price falls below the guaranteed level.

Recall one of the demand shifters: the number of buyers. Our original demand curve depicts the relationship between the price of sugar and quantity demanded by consumers, where the government is not included as a buyer. We can therefore model government purchases as an increase in the number of buyers in the sugar market: the demand curve for sugar shifts outward.

Demand shifts, but by how much? With this policy, the government does not purchase sugar indiscriminately; it buys only the amount needed to support the target price of €3 per kilo. The policy's objective pins down the exact shift in demand.

Consider a typical farmer in the EU. That farmer has no incentive to sell sugar below €3, the price at which the government promised to buy any unsold output. The same logic applies to all farmers. Therefore, the new equilibrium price must be €3. The market clears only when quantity supplied at €3 equals total quantity demanded, which now includes government purchases.

Our equilibrium condition from Module 3 becomes:

\(Q_S = Q_D + Q_{\text{GOV}}\)

where \(Q_{\text{GOV}}\) denotes the quantity purchased by government. Using the supply and demand functions from Modules 1-2, and substituting €3 for \(P\), we can find the quantity purchased by the government.

Quantity supplied at €3:
\(Q_S = 15P = 15(3) = 45\)
Consumer quantity demanded at €3:
\(Q_D = 50 - 10P = 50 - 10(3) = 20\)
Government purchases:
\(45 = 20 + Q_{\text{GOV}}\), so \(Q_{\text{GOV}} = 25\) million kg

We can visualize this as an outward shift in demand by 25 million kg. The new demand curve, which includes government as a purchaser, meets the supply curve at €3.

What to Notice

With the price support policy, consumers reduced their sugar purchases to 20 million kg. At the same time, producers increased their output to 45 million kg. These market responses are identical to those under the price floor set at €3 in Module 3. A major difference between the two policies is the fiscal cost that comes with the price support mechanism: the government purchases the entire surplus (the excess of quantity supplied over quantity demanded by consumers) at the supported price. Because all the surplus is purchased by the government, all producers are better off with this program.

Price Support, at What Cost?

Price support results in overproduction. Producers could have used their resources to produce alternative goods to meet other consumer demands. This represents a type of economic cost we will explore later in the course.

We now have all the information we need to calculate the fiscal cost of this program to the government. The government buys 25 million kg of sugar at €3/kg. Ignoring all other costs such as storage or disposal of sugar surplus, the government bears a fiscal cost of €75 million per year.

Figure 6.2: Fiscal cost of the price support equals the supported price times the government purchase quantity. This is shown by the shaded, bounded square shape.

This example illustrates why the CAP created enormous costs. The surplus is not a one-time problem: as long as the price support remains above equilibrium, surpluses continue to accumulate year after year. Multiply €75 million by the number of years the policy ran, add storage costs across multiple commodities (sugar, butter, wine, grains, dairy), and the fiscal burden becomes staggering. The CAP consumed roughly 40% of the entire EU budget for decades.

This example also illustrates a broader point about policymaking. Policy interventions rarely make everyone better off. While CAP was intended to support farmers with stable prices, the costs were borne by consumers and taxpayers.

Fiscal Cost Is Not Automatically Deadweight Loss

The €75 million fiscal cost is a taxpayer cost, but it is not automatically the same as deadweight loss. If the purchased surplus is stored, spoiled, or disposed of, much of the fiscal cost may correspond to wasted resources. If some purchased sugar is used in a way that creates value, the efficiency loss is smaller. For that reason, the graph focuses on the directly measured welfare components: private consumer surplus, producer surplus, and fiscal cost.

What Happened to the Surplus?

The purchased surplus had to go somewhere. Some agricultural surpluses were stored, some spoiled or were disposed of, and some were sold or exported at low prices. When surplus products are sold abroad at low prices, they can affect producers and consumers in other countries; that international spillover is part of the broader CAP story. Those export effects can be analyzed with the supply-shift tools from Module 4, but the central welfare point here is domestic: the price support helped EU producers, hurt private consumers, and required taxpayer spending.

Summary

The CAP price support system brings the full sequence together. Demand and supply locate the baseline equilibrium. The supported price creates a gap between private quantity demanded and producer quantity supplied. Government purchases absorb that gap and can be represented as a policy-driven outward shift in total demand.

Welfare analysis shows why this policy is contested. It supported producers, harmed private consumers, required taxpayer spending, and may have created efficiency losses depending on what happened to the purchased surplus. The point of the model is not to decide the policy question for us, but to make the trade-offs visible.
