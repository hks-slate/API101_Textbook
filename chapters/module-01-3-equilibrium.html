Module 3

Equilibrium Basics

In Modules 1 and 2 you built demand and supply curves separately. Now we bring them together: finding market equilibrium, interpreting excess supply (surplus) and excess demand (shortage), and applying these tools to a policy example.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify and solve for equilibrium price and quantity algebraically
  • Interpret market conditions when price is above or below equilibrium (surplus and shortage)
  • Graph supply and demand together and identify the equilibrium point
  • Use the supply-and-demand framework to examine a real-world policy example
Quick Recap

You already know: how demand works on the buyer side of the sugar market and how supply works on the producer side.

This module adds: the point where those two sides meet, what happens when price is above or below that point, and how that logic explains policy-driven surplus.

Market Equilibrium

What is Equilibrium?

Market Equilibrium
The price and quantity at which the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied (\(Q_D = Q_S\)). At equilibrium, there is no inherent tendency for the price to change. Graphically, equilibrium is the point where the demand and supply curves intersect.

Equilibrium is the "resting point" of a market—the unique price where the plans of consumers and producers align perfectly. At any other price, one side of the market will be frustrated: either excess demand (shortage) or excess supply (surplus). Market forces—the actions of frustrated buyers and sellers—push price toward equilibrium until balance is restored.

Markets in equilibrium tend to stay in equilibrium unless something external changes (which we will explore in Module 4). When a market is not in equilibrium, the imbalance creates incentives for buyers and sellers to adjust. If price is too high, unsold inventory accumulates, and sellers lower prices to clear it. If price is too low, buyers search desperately for products, and prices rise. This process is not always instantaneous, but the tendency is toward equilibrium.

For a price-support policy, equilibrium gives us the benchmark the policy disrupts. Without that benchmark, we cannot say whether the guaranteed price is above the market level, how large the resulting surplus is, or why the government must buy the excess output.

Finding Equilibrium Algebraically

To find the equilibrium price and quantity, set quantity demanded equal to quantity supplied and solve for the price:

Start with:
$$Q_D = Q_S$$
Substitute:
$$a - bP = c + dP$$
Collect constants:
$$a - c = dP + bP$$
Combine price terms:
$$a - c = (b + d)P$$
Solve for price:
$$P^* = \frac{a - c}{b + d}$$

Once you have found the equilibrium price \(P^*\), substitute it back into either the demand or supply equation to find the equilibrium quantity \(Q^*\):

\(Q^* = a - bP^* \quad \text{or} \quad Q^* = c + dP^*\)

Both equations should yield the same \(Q^*\). Many instructors recommend plugging the equilibrium price into both equations as a check—if you get different quantities, you have made an error.

Set \(Q_D = 50 - 10P\) equal to \(Q_S = 15P\) and solve for the equilibrium price and quantity.

Take time to generate your own response. Then show the answer to compare it with yours.

Set \(Q_D = Q_S\): \(50 - 10P = 15P\) → \(50 = 25P\) → \(P^* = 2\) €/kg.

Substitute back: \(Q^* = 15(2) = 30\). Check with demand: \(Q^* = 50 - 10(2) = 30\) ✓

Equilibrium: \(P^* = 2\) and \(Q^* = 30\) million kg/year.
What to Notice

Equilibrium is not a separate formula you memorize apart from demand and supply. It comes directly from combining the two relationships you already built in Modules 1 and 2. The logic is simple: find the price at which buyers and sellers want the same quantity, then check the quantity in both equations.

Worked Example: EU Sugar Market Equilibrium

Using our EU sugar market example, find the equilibrium price and quantity.

Given:
Demand: \(Q_D = 50 - 10P\)
Supply: \(Q_S = 15P\)
Set equal:
\(50 - 10P = 15P\)
Solve for price:
\(50 = 25P \Rightarrow P^* = 2\)
Find quantity:
\(Q^* = 50 - 10(2) = 30\)
Check:
\(Q^* = 15(2) = 30\)

The equilibrium price is €2 per kilogram, and the equilibrium quantity is 30 million kilograms per year.

Graphing Supply and Demand Together

When we graph both the demand and supply curves on the same axes, their intersection point is the equilibrium:

Inverse Demand Curve \(P = 5 - (1/10)Q_D\) Downward sloping. It crosses the price axis at \(P = 5\) and the quantity axis at \(Q = 50\).
Inverse Supply Curve \(P = (1/15)Q_S\) Upward sloping. It starts at the origin because producers supply zero when price is zero.
Equilibrium \((Q^* = 30, P^* = 2)\) The intersection point where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

Surplus and Shortage Dynamics

Surplus (Excess Supply)
When the market price is above the equilibrium price, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Producers have unsold inventory. Sellers reduce prices, creating downward pressure until the market reaches equilibrium.
Shortage (Excess Demand)
When the market price is below the equilibrium price, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. Consumers want more than producers are willing to supply. Buyers bid prices up, creating upward pressure until equilibrium is restored.

Let's use our sugar market to illustrate how the gap appears when price is away from equilibrium.

Price Quantities Market condition
\(P = 3\) \(Q_D = 50 - 10(3) = 20\)
\(Q_S = 15(3) = 45\)		 Surplus: sellers want to sell 25 million kg more than buyers want to buy.
\(P = 1\) \(Q_D = 50 - 10(1) = 40\)
\(Q_S = 15(1) = 15\)		 Shortage: buyers want 25 million kg more than sellers provide.

Common Misconception

Students often confuse "surplus" (excess of quantity supplied over quantity demanded at a given price) with "consumer surplus" or "producer surplus." Consumer surplus and producer surplus measure value created by market exchanges. A market surplus is an excess supply condition at a given price.

Common Mistake

Students often reverse surplus and shortage. The fastest way to stay oriented is to ask two questions at the chosen price: how much do buyers want, and how much do sellers want to provide? If price is above equilibrium, sellers want to sell more than buyers want to buy, so the result is a surplus. If price is below equilibrium, buyers want more than sellers provide, so the result is a shortage.

A) A shortage of 25 million kg, so prices will rise.
B) A surplus of 25 million kg, so prices will fall.
C) The market is in equilibrium at this price.
D) A shortage of 45 million kg, so prices will rise.

What to Notice

By comparing the slopes of demand and supply curves, you can tell which side of the market is more responsive to price changes.

Because price is on the vertical axis and quantity is on the horizontal axis, increasing the slope coefficient of the supply (or demand) function makes the supply (or demand) curve flatter, not steeper.

As you change the intercepts, the curves shift. As you change the slope coefficients, the curves rotate because buyers or sellers become more or less responsive to price. Notice:

Control What changes Typical equilibrium effect
Demand intercept \(a\) Shifts the demand curve to the right (higher \(a\)) or to the left (lower \(a\)). A higher \(a\) raises both equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.
Supply intercept \(c\) Shifts the supply curve to the right (higher \(c\)) or to the left (lower \(c\)). A higher \(c\) increases equilibrium quantity and lowers equilibrium price.
Slope coefficients \(b\) and \(d\) Rotate the curves by changing price responsiveness. Price and quantity can move in different directions depending on which side becomes more responsive.
If the demand equation changes to \(Q_D = 70 - 10P\) (demand intercept increases from 50 to 70) and supply stays at \(Q_S = 15P\), what is the new equilibrium price and quantity?

Take time to generate your own response. Then show the answer to compare it with yours.

Set \(70 - 10P = 15P\): \(70 = 25P\), so \(P^* = 2.8\). Then \(Q^* = 15(2.8) = 42\) million kg. Both price and quantity increased: higher demand (higher intercept) raises both the equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.
Common Mistake

When a change in the demand function's intercept shifts the demand curve, students sometimes mistakenly use the new intercept directly as the vertical-axis intercept when plotting the new demand curve. For example, if \(Q_D = 70 - 10P\), the demand curve's vertical intercept comes from the rearranged equation \(P = 7 - (1/10)Q_D\), so the correct vertical intercept is 7, not 70.

Policy Connection: Applying Equilibrium Analysis to CAP

An Alternative Policy: A Price Floor

Now that you understand demand, supply, and equilibrium, we can return to the EU Common Agricultural Policy and apply our analytical tools. Recall that the original CAP operated through price supports: the government guaranteed a minimum price for agricultural products. If the market price fell below this guaranteed price, the government purchased the unsold surplus at the guaranteed price.

There are other ways to guarantee a certain selling price for producers. A more direct approach is to regulate the prices at which market exchanges can take place. Instead of purchasing sugar at the supported price, the government could simply set a price floor for sugar. Price floors establish the minimum price in a regulated market, prohibiting any exchange below that price.

In this module, we study the effect of a price floor set above equilibrium at €3 per kg, which will help us better understand the price support system in Module 6.

Policy step Quantity calculation Result
Demand at €3 \(Q_D = 50 - 10(3) = 20\) Buyers want 20 million kg.
Supply at €3 \(Q_S = 15(3) = 45\) Producers want to sell 45 million kg.
Surplus \(45 - 20 = 25\) The price floor creates an excess supply of 25 million kg.
Figure 3.3: Government Intervention Graph Explorer. The sugar market starts at equilibrium \((Q^*, P^*) = (30, €2)\).
What to Notice

In this example, a price floor at €3 creates a surplus (excess supply) of 25 million kilograms per year. The size of the surplus, and hence a major consequence of this policy, depends on how responsive consumers and producers are to price changes. With a very steep demand curve, for example, the reduction in quantity demanded would be small, leading to a smaller surplus driven primarily by producers' response to the higher price.

Modules 1-3 Summary

In these first three modules, you built the foundational tools of supply and demand analysis: demand, supply, equilibrium, surplus, and shortage.

Demand represents how the quantity that consumers are willing to buy responds to changes in prices. The law of demand tells us that quantity demanded falls as price rises. We express demand as \(Q_D = a - bP\) and graphically as a downward-sloping curve. To graph demand, invert the equation to solve for \(P\) in terms of \(Q\). The vertical intercept shows the highest price consumers will pay; the horizontal intercept shows quantity demanded when price is zero.

Supply represents how the quantity that producers are willing to sell responds to changes in prices. The law of supply tells us that quantity supplied rises as price rises. We express supply as \(Q_S = c + dP\) and graphically as an upward-sloping curve. Like demand, invert supply to graph it with price on the vertical axis.

Equilibrium occurs where demand and supply intersect. At \(P^*\) and \(Q^*\), quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Find equilibrium by setting \(Q_D = Q_S\) and solving for \(P^*\), then substituting to find \(Q^*\). Always verify by plugging \(P^*\) into both equations.

When price is above equilibrium: surplus (excess supply). Prices fall toward equilibrium. When price is below equilibrium: shortage (excess demand). Prices rise toward equilibrium.

Supply-and-demand analysis lets us predict the consequences of a proposed policy prior to its implementation.

Understanding these shifts will enable us to examine consequences of the CAP price support mechanism more deeply.
