Understanding Supply

The analysis in this unit applies to firms operating in competitive markets — markets in which many sellers offer identical products and no individual firm can influence the market price. Because each competitive firm takes the market price as given, its only decision is how much to produce. This leads to a well-defined relationship between the price and quantity supplied: the market supply curve. For firms operating in other market structures (e.g., monopoly and oligopoly), price is no longer fixed from the firm's perspective, and the supply curve as a concept no longer applies in the same way. Those settings require different models of production and pricing decisions, which we will take up later in the course.

The Law of Supply

Law of Supply
Holding all else equal (ceteris paribus), when the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises; when the price falls, the quantity supplied falls. The supply curve slopes upward from left to right.

Supply describes the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity that producers are willing and able to offer for sale for a given period of time. Unlike the inverse relationship of demand, supply slopes upward: higher prices create stronger incentives for producers to increase output. When the price of a good rises, producers find it profitable to expand output—by hiring more workers, running facilities longer, or diverting resources from lower-value uses. When price falls, those incentives weaken and producers scale back.

Just as with demand, we can represent supply with a supply schedule (a table) or a supply curve (a graph). The upward slope reflects the law of supply: producers supply more at higher prices. This reflects economic reality: firms have costs of production, and higher prices allow them to cover those costs and earn profits even when expanding production.

In the CAP setting, this is the other half of the story: if the policy guarantees a higher sugar price, farmers respond by producing more. Module 3 will show why combining that upward-sloping supply curve with the downward-sloping demand curve creates excess supply (surplus), and Module 6 will return to the full CAP price-support system.

Supply Equations and Interpretation

Linear supply relationships typically take the form:

$$Q_S = c + dP$$

Quantity supplied equals the baseline quantity producers bring to market, plus the additional quantity they supply as price rises.

\(Q_S\) Outcome

The quantity supplied at a particular price. On the graph, this is read on the horizontal axis.

\(c\) Quantity Intercept

The constant term: the baseline quantity producers supply when price is zero. This may be zero or negative.

\(d\) Price Response

How many units quantity supplied rises when price increases by one unit. Larger values mean producers respond more sharply.

\(P\) Price

The price producers receive. In economics graphs, price is placed on the vertical axis.

The positive sign captures the direct relationship. It turns a higher price into a higher quantity supplied, which is exactly the law of supply.

Building a Supply Schedule: The EU Sugar Market

Continuing with our EU sugar market, suppose economists have estimated the supply equation for sugar beet farmers:

\(Q_S = 15P\)

Where \(Q_S\) is measured in millions of kilograms per year and \(P\) is the price in euros per kilogram. Notice that \(c = 0\) and is omitted in the equation: \(Q_S = 15P\). At a price of zero, producers supply nothing — which makes economic sense. Note: this is a stylized illustration — the numbers are chosen to keep the algebra clean, not to match actual EU market volumes or intervention prices.

Read the Equation in Words

If sugar were priced at zero, sellers would supply zero units. For every €1 increase in price, producers increase the quantity supplied by 15 million kg.

Slope of the supply function is 15. Recall that the slope of the demand function is 10. Which side of the market, supply or demand, is more sensitive to price changes?
The supply function is steeper than the demand function (\(15 > 10\)), which implies that producers are more sensitive to price changes. For each €1 increase in price, quantity supplied increases by 15 million kilograms, whereas it reduces quantity demanded by 10 million kilograms.
Price (€/kg) Quantity Supplied — \(Q_S = 15P\) (millions of kg/year)
0 15(0) = 0
1 15(1) = 15
2 15(2) = 30
3 15(3) = 45
4 15(4) = 60
5 15(5) = 75

Notice the pattern: for each €1 increase in price, quantity supplied increases by 15 million kilograms. The supply curve is perfectly linear, reflecting constant price responsiveness across all prices.

What to Notice

The schedule makes the law of supply visible in the same way the demand table did in Module 1. Every €1 increase in price brings 15 million more kilograms of sugar to market. In CAP terms, this is why a guaranteed high price encourages overproduction rather than simply preserving the old output level.

If the supply equation is \(Q_S = 15P\), how many millions of kilograms are supplied when the price is €3 per kilogram?
Substitute \(P = 3\): \(Q_S = 15(3) = 45\) million kilograms per year.

From Equation to Graph: Inverting the Supply Equation

To graph the supply curve with price on the vertical axis, we invert the supply equation so that P is expressed in terms of quantity:

Start with:
$$Q_S = 15P$$
Divide both sides by 15:
$$P = (1/15)Q_S$$

The inverted form is \(P = (1/15)Q_S\). Two features tell us how to draw the line:

Feature Value How to read it
Vertical intercept \(P = 0\) When \(Q_S = 0\), the curve crosses the price axis at zero. The supply curve passes through the origin.
Slope \(1/15\) For every 15-million-kg increase in quantity, price increases by €1.
What to Notice

Supply is simpler to invert here than demand because the equation has no constant term: \(Q_S = 15P\) becomes \(P = (1/15)Q_S\). That immediately tells you two things students should internalize: the curve passes through the origin, and its slope is positive.

Step-by-Step: Graphing the Supply Curve

Because the supply curve in this example starts from the origin (when \(P = 0\), we have \(Q_S = 0\)), graphing it is even more straightforward than demand. Two methods work well:

Method 1

Origin and Slope

The supply curve passes through the origin (0, 0) — because \(c = 0\) in our equation, meaning producers supply nothing when the price is zero. This gives us a free starting point that requires no calculation. From there, apply the slope: move right 15, up 1 repeatedly until you cross the domain.

Important Distinction: Supply vs. Quantity Supplied

"Supply" refers to the entire curve — the full relationship between price and quantity supplied. "Quantity supplied" refers to a specific point on that curve at a particular price. When the supply curve shifts (which we will explore in Module 4), economists say "supply increased" or "supply decreased." Movements along the supply curve due to price changes are changes in "quantity supplied," not changes in "supply."

Common Mistake

Two errors show up repeatedly here. First, students sometimes draw supply downward because they are still thinking about demand. Second, they say "supply increased" when price rises. A price rise means a movement along the supply curve, so only quantity supplied increases.

Policy Connection: Supply in the CAP Sugar Market

In the EU sugar market, the supply equation \(Q_S = 15P\) reflects the behavior of European sugar beet farmers: higher prices induce more production. The supply curve starts from the origin, meaning no sugar is produced at a price of zero. This simple linear supply equation will underpin our analysis of equilibrium in Module 3 and the CAP price support in Module 6.

In the EU sugar supply equation \(Q_S = 15P\), the slope coefficient \(d = 15\). What does this number tell you about sugar beet farmers as a group — and how would a larger value of \(d\) change your interpretation of their behavior?
\(d = 15\) means that for every €1 increase in price, farmers collectively supply 15 million more kilograms per year. A larger \(d\) would mean producers are more responsive to price signals: the same price increase triggers a much larger increase in output. This could reflect things like easily expandable farmland, low costs of switching between crops, or a large number of farms that can pivot quickly. A smaller \(d\) means the same price increase yields little extra output — perhaps because production capacity is fixed in the short run.
