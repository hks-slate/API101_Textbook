Module 4

Demand & Supply Shifters

Beyond price: understanding the forces that move entire demand and supply curves and reshape market equilibria.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify the key factors that shift the demand curve
  • Identify the key factors that shift the supply curve
  • Distinguish between normal goods and inferior goods
  • Explain how substitute and complementary goods affect demand
  • Model a demand or supply shift algebraically and graphically
  • Apply the four-step process to analyze equilibrium changes
  • Predict price and quantity changes when curves shift

Introduction

In Modules 1-3, you learned how the law of demand and law of supply create the downward-sloping demand curve and upward-sloping supply curve, then how those curves determine equilibrium. Both relationships hold true under a critical assumption: ceteris paribus, or "all else equal." But in the real world, "all else" is constantly changing. Consumer tastes shift, production technologies advance, input costs rise and fall, and new competitors enter or leave markets.

This module extends your understanding of markets by examining what happens when those other factors change. You will learn which factors cause demand curves to shift, which cause supply curves to shift, and how these shifts propagate through markets to create new equilibria. This is the heart of economic policy analysis: understanding how interventions, shocks, or natural events reshape market outcomes.

As in Modules 1-3, we will keep using agricultural-market examples so the mechanics stay concrete. The CAP price support system returns in Module 6, after you have practiced the standard tools for modeling demand and supply shifters.

Critical Distinction

Movement along a curve: A change in the price of the good itself causes a movement along the demand or supply curve. The curve itself does not move.

Shift of the curve: A change in any other factor (income, tastes, input costs, technology, etc.) shifts the entire curve to the left or right. This is a fundamentally different effect on the market.

Factors That Shift Demand

A demand curve represents the quantity consumers are willing and able to buy at each price, holding everything else constant. When any of those other factors change, the entire curve shifts — meaning at every price level, a different quantity is demanded. Understanding which factors shift demand is essential for predicting how markets respond to real-world changes.

Demand Shifters
Factors that cause the entire demand curve to shift to a new position:
  • Consumer Income: How much money households have to spend
  • Tastes and Preferences: Consumer attitudes toward the good
  • Prices of Substitutes: Prices of goods that can replace this good
  • Prices of Complements: Prices of goods used together with this good
  • Expectations: Beliefs about future prices or income
  • Number of Buyers: Size and composition of the market

Normal Goods vs. Inferior Goods

Not all goods respond to income changes in the same way. When consumer income rises, demand for most goods increases — these are called normal goods. Examples include restaurant meals, new cars, and vacation travel. The demand curve shifts to the right: at any price, consumers want to buy more.

For a few goods, the opposite happens. When income rises, demand decreases — these are inferior goods. Classic examples are generic-brand products, used cars, or bus rides. As people become wealthier, they buy less of these goods and switch to higher-quality alternatives. The demand curve shifts to the left.

Normal Good
A good whose demand increases when consumer income rises and decreases when income falls.
Inferior Good
A good whose demand decreases when consumer income rises and increases when income falls. This label describes a relationship between the good and income, not a judgment about the good's quality.

Substitutes and Complements

The prices of other goods in the market strongly influence demand for any particular good. Goods can be related in two key ways:

Substitute Goods
Goods that consumers can use in place of each other. When the price of a substitute rises, demand for the original good increases (consumers switch to it). When the price of a substitute falls, demand for the original good decreases (consumers switch out of it).
Complementary Goods
Goods that are typically consumed together. When the price of a complement rises, demand for the original good decreases (fewer people buy both). When the price of a complement falls, demand for the original good increases (more people buy both).

Example: Substitutes and Complements

Related goods change demand by changing how attractive the original good feels at every price. If a substitute becomes more expensive, buyers switch to the original good and its demand shifts right. If a complement becomes more expensive, so does the original good, and its demand shifts left.

Factors That Shift Supply

Just as demand curves shift when underlying conditions change, so do supply curves. A supply curve shows the quantity firms are willing to produce and sell at each price. When the cost or feasibility of production changes, the entire curve shifts. Producers will offer more of a good if it becomes cheaper or easier to make, and less if it becomes more expensive or difficult.

Supply Shifters
Factors that cause the entire supply curve to shift to a new position:
  • Input Prices: Costs of labor, materials, energy, and other production factors
  • Technology: Methods and efficiency of production
  • Natural Conditions: Weather, natural disasters, or resource availability
  • Producer Expectations: Beliefs about future prices or conditions
  • Number of Sellers: How many firms are in the market

How Changes in Production Costs Affect Supply

Firms make production decisions based on profit — the difference between revenue and costs. When production costs fall, firms can produce the same quantity at lower cost, making previously unprofitable production economically viable. Supply increases and the supply curve shifts right. When production costs rise, supply decreases and the supply curve shifts left.

Technology and Productivity

When firms discover new production methods or technologies that reduce costs, supply increases dramatically. A classic example is the Green Revolution of the 1960s and 1970s, when improved seeds for wheat and rice doubled crop yields in low-income countries. Supply curves for these crops shifted right, and more food became available at every price level.

Illustrating Supply Shifts

Two common supply shifters move the supply curve in opposite directions. Bad weather makes production harder and shifts supply left. Better technology makes production easier and shifts supply right.

Figure 4.2: Drought or bad weather shifts supply left.

Figure 4.3: Better technology shifts supply right.

Exceptionally hot weather and very little rainfall reduce agricultural yields. What happens to the supply curve for sugar?
The supply curve for sugar shifts to the left (decreases). Poor weather makes production more difficult and reduces output at every price. This is a negative supply shock — different from a movement along the supply curve, which would occur only if sugar's own price changed.
Important Principle

A shift in one curve never causes a shift in the other curve. Instead, a shift in one curve causes a movement along the other curve. For example, if supply decreases (curve shifts left), the equilibrium price rises, causing a movement up and to the left along the demand curve. But the demand curve itself does not shift.

Summary

Module 4 expanded your toolkit for understanding markets. Price is not the only factor affecting markets; changes in income, tastes, technology, input costs, and expectations drive shifts in demand and/or supply. The critical distinction is between movements along curves (caused by price changes) and shifts of curves (caused by other factors).

You also practiced the four-step process: identify the event, decide which curve shifts, determine the direction of the shift, and compare the new equilibrium to the old one. Module 6 will use these same tools to analyze the CAP price support system as a policy-driven market intervention.
